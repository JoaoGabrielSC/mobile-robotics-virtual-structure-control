Introducing the Dynamic Model
The basic assumption when dealing with the kinematic model of all the robots we have discussed in the previous chapter is that the robot immediately develops the commanded velocity. This means that the vehicle does not have mass/inertia, or it has the capability of infinite acceleration, both impossible. 

In other words, it takes some time to the velocity developed by the vehicle to reach the commanded value, due to the mass/inertia and finite acceleration capability. The way the real velocity approaches the commanded one is dependent of the maximum available acceleration, what is associated to a dynamic behavior of the vehicle (dynamic because it involves acceleration, or force/torque).

We can consider that a change in the commanded velocity is a velocity step applied to the robot, and we should consider its step response. In a certain way the dynamic model of the vehicle shows how this step response varies along time.

As a consequence, right way after changing the commanded velocity there will be an error between the current velocity and the desired one, characterizing a velocity tracking error. 

Therefore, it is quite important to model the dynamic behavior of the robot.

--- Slide 3 ---
To reduce such errors it is necessary to consider the dynamics of the robot, or its mass and inertia, and, thus, its finite linear and angular accelerations.

Control of Multi-robot Systems
Controlling a multi-robot system, also known as a cluster or formation of mobile robots, involves coordinating a group of robots to collaborate on specific tasks. 
This type of control is not merely about managing each robot independently but ensuring that each one acts to contribute to the overall task at hand. 
Examples of such formations can be observed in various scenarios such as load transportation, inspecting disaster areas for survivors, surveillance of large areas, such as country borders, locating and disarming landmines, and even in competitive robot soccer matches.

--- Slide 3 ---
Control of Multi-robot Systems

--- Slide 4 ---
Several tasks accomplished by mobile robots are better accomplished by a group of robots working cooperatively than using a single robot. This is due, some times, to the own nature of the task to be accomplished, or even to the cost of accomplishing the task, when accomplished by a single robot.
In the previous examples one can clearly see cases in which the task to be accomplished is better accomplished when a group of robots is adopted, instead of a single one. Let us see some cases:
Case the bar were transported by a single drone such a robot would certainly be much greater, given the length of the piece, and it is much likely it would be more expensive than the two ones adopted.
Regarding the example of inspection of rubble areas looking for survivors, using a group of robots the inspection task of a given area would be completed in much less time, thus increasing the probability of finding live victims.
Control of Multi-robot Systems
When it comes to locating and disarming mines, there is a significant risk of the inspection robot being destroyed by an explosion. Utilizing a fleet of less expensive robots, instead of relying on a single high-capacity unit, not only enables a broader coverage area in the same timeframe but also proves to be more economically beneficial. This approach minimizes the financial impact of losing a robot since it would not incur substantial costs or disrupt the ongoing task.
Packages delivery requires a ground vehicle from which the delivery drone takes off to deliver a package and on which it lands after delivering the package.

--- Slide 5 ---
Meaningful remarks:
1) Regarding a task of searching and deactivation of landmines, there is a great risk of loosing the inspecting robot, for the risk of an explosion. Therefore, using a group of cheaper robots instead of a single one of high inspection capability not only allows covering a greater area in the same timeframe, but also is economically more advantageous: loosing a robot would not represent great cost and would not halt the task.
2) In the case of the search for survivors in rubble areas, by its turn, time is an important variable. The faster the search is executed, the greater the chance of finding survivors. Therefore, using more robots allows to accomplish the task with more efficiency.
3) Eventually, it is possible to reduce cost, since a group of simpler robots can be cheaper than a single robot of more capacity, as it is the case in landmines detection.
Control of Multi-robot Systems

--- Slide 6 ---
In the formation control there are two relevant aspects: 
the motion control of each robot in the cluster, and 
the coordination between their movements, to preserve the formation shape along navigation.
Summarizing, the formation control is a coordinated control of the individual movement of each robot in the formation.
Control of Multi-robot Systems

--- Slide 7 ---
Among the approaches for the coordinated control of mobile robots three distinct techniques are constantly referred to. They are the leader-follower, the virtual structure, and the behavior-based approaches.

In the leader-follower approach a robot defined as the leader is responsible for guiding all the other robots compounding the formation, to take them to their desired positions and keep the shape of the formation along navigation. Normally, also, all the sensing responsibility and the generation of the control signals sent to each member of the formation is attributed to the leader. Therefore, in such an approach the leader is the most important agent, and its failure would compromise the task accomplishment, even halting the task.
Control of Multi-robot Systems

--- Slide 8 ---
In the case of the virtual structure approach the main agent becomes the whole formation, with is dealt with as a single rigid body, virtual, with movement capability. The individual robots in the cluster move to establish or to keep a predefined geometric figure. In such a case the figure of the leader does not exist anymore, once the whole formation is considered as a single robot. In particular, the whole formation is considered as a single virtual robot, thus massless, and thus without any dynamic effect. Therefore, it is enough to design a kinematic controller for the virtual robot, to control the movement of the whole formation.
By its turn, when adopting the behavior-based approach a set of behaviors is defined to each robot in the team, such as to search for a target point, to avoid obstacles and to establish the formation, for instance. The control signals sent to each robot can correspond either to a weighted average of each behavior, for instance, or it can exist an hierarchy among the behaviors, to be followed in the control.
Control of Multi-robot Systems

--- Slide 9 ---
In terms of the implementation of the control system, it can be centralized or decentralized. 

For the case of a centralized control structure, the element responsible for executing the control algorithm of the formation (it can be a robot of the formation or an external computer) is also responsible for gathering all the information related to the group, such as their relative (or absolute) positions, and for sending the respective control signals to establish/move the formation. 
On the other hand, a decentralized control structure would not have such a centralizing unit. In such a way, each robot in the team has its own sensors, to inform its current position in the environment, the current state of the working environment and the current positions of the other members of the cluster, such that it works to assembly/move the formation.
Control of Multi-robot Systems

--- Slide 10 ---
In both cases it is necessary a two-way communication channel
between each robot and the centralizing unit, in the case of a centralized control structure, so that each robot inform its position to the centralizing unit and receive the control signals sent by it;
between each robot and all the others, when a decentralized control structure is adopted, so that each robot can receive the information about the positions of all the members of the group  and inform its own position to all the partners.

Notice that this (the communication network) can become a bottleneck when the formation involves several robots, mainly in the case of decentralized control.

Leader-Follower Paradigm
A leader-follower formation is configured as two robots, one of them, the follower, replicating the movement of the other, the leader.  Therefore, the leader in motion configures a trajectory the follower should track. 
However, there is a certain offset between the position of the leader, the desired position for the follower, and the real position of the follower. 
This way, two controllers should be designed: one for the leader robot and one for the follower robot. In some applications the leader can be guided by a human operator, like the delivery truck in a parcel delivery application, even remotely, but it could also be autonomously controlled. 
An important aspect is that the leader robot can be accomplishing any task (positioning, path-following, trajectory-tracking), but the follower should be controlled to track the trajectory characterized by the leader in motion. 
Notice that such a configuration is quite interesting when two robots should transport a bar shaped load, for the nature of the application itself. 
However, a meaningful drawback associated to this multi-robot system is that the leader robot can not stop moving. Whenever it stops moving the follower also stops moving, since it replicates the movement of the leader. This way, if the leader undesirably stops working the entire formation will stop, halting the task.

Virtual Structure - Multiple Control Objectives
When a given task is proposed, it is common that it can be split in some subtasks. For instance, when navigating a virtual structure at least two subtasks can be identified: to move the formation and to keep its shape/orientation. 
The virtual paradigm so far discussed considers these two subtasks as a single task, not prioritizing any of the two subtasks. Other example is the case in which the virtual structure should avoid an obstacle. In such a case it is common that the obstacle avoidance subtask have highest priority, to prevent any accident.
Therefore, it is sometimes necessary to split the task to be accomplished into some minor subtasks, whose accomplishment result in the accomplishment of the whole task, assigning different priorities to them. In this case, subtasks of lower priority will be executed if not conflicting with the subtasks of higher priority. This means that the task of moving a formation to follow a given path will be executed just in the absence of obstacles, supposing that there is a subtask of obstacle avoidance with higher priority.

Virtual Structure - Multiple Control Objectives
Considering the three subtasks to move the formation, to avoid obstacles and to preserve the formation shape, two distinct situations can be observed, regarding the priorities assigned to the subtasks. They are what we call rigid formation and flexible formation. In the first case the formation shape is preserved when deviating from the obstacle, and in the second case the formation is deformed when avoiding an obstacle. 
For the rigid formation, the sequence of priorities, from the highest to the lowest, are to preserve the formation shape, to avoid an obstacle and to move the formation.
For the flexible formation, such a sequence is deviate from the obstacle, preserve the formation shape and move the formation.
